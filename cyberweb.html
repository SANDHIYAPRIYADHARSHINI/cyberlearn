Phishing Attacks

A phishing attack is a type of cybercrime where attackers try to trick people into giving away sensitive information like passwords, credit card numbers, bank details, personal information, and login credentials (email, social media, etc.). They do this by pretending to be someone the user might trust — like a bank or a popular company.

How Does a Phishing Attack Work?

The attacker sends a fake message (email, SMS, social media, etc.) as if it was sent from a trusted sender to make the user click a link or attachment. This takes the user to a fake site, or malware is downloaded — then information is stolen, or the user's device gets infected.

Types of Phishing Attacks

  • Phishing by Email

    Attackers send fake emails pretending to be from a trusted source (e.g., banks, Amazon, Gmail) to trick users into clicking malicious links or giving up credentials.

    Example: In 2021, attackers impersonated Microsoft and sent emails warning users that their Outlook accounts would be deactivated due to inactivity. The message included a link to "verify your account," which led to a fake login page designed to steal credentials. The email looked official, complete with Microsoft branding and urgent language.

  • Smishing (SMS Phishing)

    Phishing messages sent via text messages to trick users into clicking a link, calling a fake number, or installing malware.

    Example: In 2020, scammers sent fake SMS messages claiming to be from FedEx, saying a package was waiting for delivery and urging recipients to click a link to "reschedule." The link led to a phishing site that asked for personal details and credit card information.

  • Pop-up Phishing

    A deceptive cyberattack that uses fake pop-up messages — often disguised as virus alerts or software updates — to trick users into clicking malicious links or downloading malware. Common on compromised websites or triggered by adware, these pop-ups may also urge users to call fake support numbers.

    Example: In 2017, users browsing the web were hit with fake pop-up alerts claiming their computers were infected with viruses. These pop-ups mimicked Microsoft security warnings, urging users to call a "support number" or download antivirus software. In reality, the number connected them to scammers who tried to extract payment or remote access to their devices.

  • Vishing (Voice Phishing)

    Voice calls pretending to be from banks, tech support, etc., used to extract sensitive info like OTPs, card details, or convince victims to install remote access tools.

    Example: In 2019, a UK-based energy firm was scammed out of $243,000 when attackers used AI voice cloning to impersonate the company's German CEO. The executive received a call that sounded exactly like his boss, urgently requesting a wire transfer to a Hungarian supplier. Believing it was legitimate, he approved the payment — only to discover it was fraud.

  • Commodity Phishing-as-a-Service (PhaaS)

    AI-driven phishing tools that require no technical expertise to craft spoofed emails and fake pages.

    Example: In 2022, the platform 16Shop sold phishing kits impersonating brands like Amazon and PayPal. These kits included fake login pages, automated IP filtering to dodge security tools, and Telegram integration to notify attackers when victims submitted credentials. Buyers could subscribe monthly or purchase one-off kits — making phishing attacks as easy as online shopping.

  • Spear Phishing

    A highly targeted phishing attack aimed at a specific person or organisation, used to gain access to confidential data or systems.

    Example: In 2011, cybersecurity firm RSA Security was hit by a targeted spear phishing attack. Employees received emails titled "2011 Recruitment Plan" with an Excel file containing a zero-day exploit. When opened, it installed a backdoor, allowing attackers to steal sensitive data related to RSA's SecurID authentication tokens.

  • Whaling

    A form of spear phishing targeting executives or high-level personnel like CEOs, CFOs, or directors — used to steal company secrets or authorise fake transactions.

    Example: In 2016, toy giant Mattel nearly lost $3 million when a finance executive received an email appearing to be from the newly appointed CEO, requesting an urgent wire transfer to a Chinese vendor. The email was fake — but convincing enough to bypass internal checks. Luckily, the funds were recovered just in time.

  • Clone Phishing

    A legitimate email is copied and a malicious version is sent in its place — looking almost identical — but with altered links or attachments.

    Example: In 2021, attackers cloned a legitimate email from DocuSign, a widely used e-signature service. They resent it with a note saying, "Updated document attached due to formatting issue." The attachment looked identical — but was malware. Because recipients had previously received similar emails, many trusted it and clicked without hesitation.

  • Angler Phishing

    Conducted via social media platforms using fake support accounts or comments to steal credentials or redirect users to phishing sites.

    Example: In 2023, scammers created fake customer support accounts on Twitter/X impersonating banking institutions. When frustrated users tweeted complaints, the attackers replied with links to "resolve the issue." These links led to phishing sites that stole login credentials and personal data.

  • Business Email Compromise (BEC)

    A cyberattack where threat actors impersonate trusted individuals — like vendors, executives, or colleagues — to trick employees into sending money or revealing sensitive data. Often involves email spoofing or compromised business email accounts.

    Example: In 2019, tech giants Facebook and Google were tricked into sending over $121 million to a scammer who impersonated a legitimate vendor. The attacker sent fake invoices and contracts via email, and employees — believing the requests were genuine — approved the wire transfers.

  • Watering Hole Phishing

    Cybercriminals target specific individuals through websites they visit regularly, like industry forums, internal portals, or even food platforms.

    Example: In 2015, a Chinese hacking group targeted the Forbes website. They exploited zero-day vulnerabilities in Internet Explorer and Adobe Flash Player to inject malicious code into Forbes' "Thought of the Day" feature. When users visited the site, the malware silently infected vulnerable devices.

  • Pharming

    Redirects users from a real website to a fake one, often using DNS poisoning, to steal credentials without the victim noticing the switch.

    Example: In 2008, a Mexican bank was hit by a drive-by pharming attack. Hackers sent emails with hidden code that silently changed the DNS settings on users' home routers. When customers tried to access the bank's website, they were redirected to a fake version — and unknowingly handed over their login credentials.
